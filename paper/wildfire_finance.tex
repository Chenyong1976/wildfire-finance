\begin{abstract}
\noindent We estimate the causal effect of wildfire exposure on county government fiscal
outcomes along two margins: the \textit{extensive margin} (fire incidence---any
qualifying wildfire $\geq\!1{,}000$ acres) and the \textit{intensive margin} (fire
severity---detectable stand-replacement burning). County governments in fire-prone
areas budget for expected wildfire costs; routine fires remain within these planned
commitments. When a fire becomes unexpectedly intense, however, the resources
required can exceed pre-committed funds, generating fiscal stress. We test whether
fiscal effects therefore depend on severity, not merely incidence. Using quinquennial
Census of Governments data from 2002 to 2022 for all county governments in the
contiguous United States ($n = 2{,}975$), we implement staggered
difference-in-differences designs with Callaway and Sant'Anna (2021) doubly-robust
estimation, exploiting the 2012 USFS Wildfire Potential raster as a predetermined
matching variable. The extensive margin yields uniformly insignificant effects across
all fiscal outcomes. At the intensive margin---384 counties with high-severity fires
compared to 2,591 never-treated controls (effective sample size: 122.1)---capital
expenditures fall by \$\!-293 per capita ($p = 0.075$, C\&S doubly-robust) and
\$\!-87 per capita ($p = 0.049$, Sun-Abraham); all other fiscal outcomes, including
fiscal balance, are statistically indistinguishable from zero. The national average
masks substantial heterogeneity: western states show a fiscal balance decline of
\$\!-1{,}252 ($p = 0.002$) and revenue losses of \$\!-1{,}063 ($p = 0.017$),
and excluding California eliminates the capital expenditure finding. Property tax
revenue declines concentrate in Dillon's Rule states (\$\!-410, $p = 0.011$) and
are absent in home rule counties, identifying counties' statutory taxing authority
as a determinant of whether wildfire reduces property tax revenues.
\medskip

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} wildfire; fire severity; local public finance; fiscal federalism;
home rule; Dillon's Rule; difference-in-differences; contiguous United States

\noindent\textbf{JEL:} H72, Q54, H71, R11
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
%% ============================================================

Wildfire poses a recurring fiscal challenge to county governments. Property tax
revenues may fall as fire damages assessed values, while demands for emergency
response, infrastructure repair, and public services rise simultaneously. County
governments in fire-prone areas are not uniformly caught off guard: they accumulate
experience with wildfire and maintain dedicated reserves and mutual-aid agreements
to cover anticipated response costs. A routine fire, however large by absolute
standards, is therefore unlikely to breach these planned commitments. What generates
fiscal stress is unexpected severity---fires that cause stand-replacement burning
across thousands of acres require suppression, evacuation, and rebuilding commitments
that can exceed a county's pre-committed resources by a substantial margin. Whether
fiscal effects consequently depend on fire severity, rather than mere incidence, is a
central question for state and local fiscal policy, yet causal evidence for county
governments outside California is limited. Between 2013 and 2021, 884 of 2,975
county governments across the contiguous United States experienced at least one
qualifying wildfire ($\geq\!1{,}000$ acres); among these, 384 experienced a fire
classified by the USFS Monitoring Trends in Burn Severity (MTBS) program as
producing stand-replacement burning.

This paper provides that evidence along two distinct margins for the full
contiguous United States. The \textit{extensive margin} asks whether experiencing
any qualifying wildfire ($\geq\!1{,}000$ acres) affects county fiscal outcomes;
the 884 fire-affected counties are compared to 2,091 counties that experienced
no qualifying fire. The \textit{intensive margin} asks whether fire
\textit{severity}---holding fire occurrence approximately fixed---amplifies the
fiscal effect; the 384 counties with high-severity fires are compared to 2,591
counties without a qualifying high-severity fire (including counties that
experienced only low-to-moderate-severity fires). We use quinquennial Census of Governments data from 2002 to 2022 for all county
governments in the lower-48 states, covering 2,975 counties across five census
years (14,875 observations).

Identification in both designs exploits the predetermined 2012 USFS Wildfire
Potential (WFP) raster as a matching variable in a staggered
difference-in-differences (DiD) framework. The primary estimator is Callaway and
Sant'Anna's \citeyearpar{callaway2021} doubly-robust group-time estimator.
Within a given wildfire hazard class, the timing of a county's first large
fire in 2013--2021 depends on stochastic weather shocks---drought, lightning,
and wind---that are plausibly orthogonal to fiscal trends conditional on
baseline hazard and demographic covariates. Pre-treatment parallel trends are verified for both designs. As robustness checks for the intensive-margin
specification, we implement Sun and Abraham's \citeyearpar{sun2021} TWFE estimator,
Arkhangelsky et al.'s \citeyearpar{arkhangelsky2021} Synthetic DiD, and
Gardner's \citeyearpar{gardner2022} two-stage DiD.

Capital expenditures decline at the intensive margin; all other fiscal outcomes,
including fiscal balance, total revenue, and property tax revenue, are
statistically indistinguishable from zero at the national level. The extensive
margin shows no significant effects on any outcome. For the intensive margin,
capital outlays fall by \$\!-293 per capita (SE\,=\,164; $p = 0.075$) under C\&S
doubly-robust estimation. Sun and Abraham's heterogeneity-robust TWFE estimator
yields a consistent but smaller decline (\$\!-87, SE\,=\,44; $p = 0.049$).
Synthetic DiD and Two-Stage DiD produce near-zero estimates (\$\!-3 and \$\!-25,
both statistically insignificant). The divergence across estimators reflects a
difference in how the control group is weighted: C\&S applies doubly-robust
propensity-score reweighting that up-weights high-wildfire-potential counties,
concentrating comparisons among counties with similar fire risk; Synthetic DiD
and Two-Stage DiD weight all never-treated counties equally, averaging in the
predominantly unaffected eastern majority and attenuating the estimate toward
zero. The effective sample size is 122.1 reweighted counties out of 2,591
never-treated controls.

Two subgroup analyses reveal that the national average conceals opposing institutional
responses. Restricting to western states yields a fiscal balance decline of
\$\!-1{,}252 ($p = 0.002$) and a revenue decline of \$\!-1{,}063 ($p = 0.017$),
absent in the national average, which includes the predominantly unaffected eastern
majority. A separate institutional heterogeneity analysis shows that the property
tax revenue decline is concentrated in Dillon's Rule states (\$\!-410, SE\,=\,161,
$p = 0.011$), where counties lack discretionary authority to adjust tax levies after
wildfire-induced assessed value erosion; home rule counties show no detectable effect
(\$\!-26, SE\,=\,242, $p = 0.915$). Counties' statutory taxing authority thus
determines whether wildfire translates into property tax revenue losses.

This paper makes three contributions. First, it provides causal estimates of wildfire
fiscal effects for county governments across the contiguous United States, extending
\citet{liao2022} from California municipalities to a national panel with diverse
property tax institutions and fiscal regimes. The capital expenditure decline of
\$\!-293 per capita is detected under C\&S doubly-robust and Sun-Abraham estimators
against an effective control pool of 122.1 reweighted counties; near-zero estimates
from Synthetic DiD and Two-Stage DiD arise because those methods do not reweight the
control pool toward comparable high-fire-risk counties. Second, it documents
geographic heterogeneity: western states experience a fiscal balance decline of
\$\!-1{,}252 per capita ($p = 0.002$) and a revenue decline of \$\!-1{,}063
($p = 0.017$), absent in the national average, with California identified as the
primary driver of the national capital expenditure result. Third, it shows that
Dillon's Rule counties experience property tax revenue losses of \$\!-410 per capita
($p = 0.011$) following high-severity wildfire while home rule counties show no
such decline, identifying counties' statutory taxing authority as a determinant of
how wildfire affects local revenues.

The paper proceeds as follows. Section~2 reviews the related literature.
Section~3 presents the data and both treatment definitions. Section~4 details
the empirical strategy. Section~5 reports main results for both margins,
including dose-response analysis and institutional heterogeneity by fiscal
legal doctrine. Section~6 presents robustness checks. Section~7 concludes.

\section{Related Literature}
%% ============================================================

Natural disasters impose persistent fiscal costs on local governments through multiple channels: tax base
erosion, elevated spending demands, and federal aid crowding. However, the existing studies focus almost 
entirely on hurricanes and floods. \citet{deryugina2017} showed that
post-hurricane government transfers to affected counties exceed direct disaster aid
in present value, revealing the shock absorption role of the traditional social safety net programs.
\citet{jerch2023} analyzed the hurricane impact on municipal governments in US Atlantic states. They found that major
hurricanes reduced local tax revenues and raised debt costs persistently for over a
decade, with effects concentrated in municipalities with a one standard deviation-above-average racial minority composition. Neither paper
examines wildfire. The wildfire context differs in three critical ways: damage centers
on forest land and vegetation rather than residential structure stock; federal aid
architecture (FEMA declarations are less automatic) limits federal offset to
own-source losses; and affected geography (rural western counties with thin property
tax bases) creates different revenue elasticities than Atlantic and Gulf Coast
communities.

The existing literature on the economics of wildfires tend to take the environmental and resource economics 
perspective as reviewed by \citet{bayham2022}. Causal research on wildfires has concentrated on
property values and residential development \citep{baylis2023}, health outcomes
from smoke exposure \citep{borgschulte2024}, and insurance markets \citep{boomhower2023}.
To our knowledge, \citet{liao2022} provides the only causal estimate of wildfire fiscal
impacts: using quasi-random fire perimeter exposure within California, they found
that municipalities experienced a 25-percentage-point increase in the probability
of running a budget deficit post-fire, with expenditures growing 17.3\% against
revenues growing 10.5\%, generating a net fiscal loss of approximately \$97 per
capita. Their setting, however, is California-specific in two important ways.
First, Proposition 13 creates a unique property tax dynamic: assessed values are
frozen at purchase price and only reset upon sale, which means post-fire property
sales generate reassessment events that temporarily raise assessed values and
transfer tax revenues even as fire-damaged properties lose market value. Second,
their sample is municipal governments, which operate under different revenue
authorities than counties and concentrate in high-density coastal and suburban
California rather than rural forested communities exposed to wildfire in the interior
west. Our analysis moves to county governments
across the contiguous United States, including states without Proposition~13
constraints on assessed values.

\textbf{Property tax dynamics.} Property tax revenues lag market value changes through
two mechanisms: institutional assessment cycles (annual, two-, or three-year
reassessment schedules) and assessor discretion in translating market losses to
assessed values. \citet{lutz2011} documents a four-year lag between housing-market
troughs and property tax revenue troughs during the 2008 crisis.
\citet{chen2024} shows that a 1\% market value decline translates to less than
0.30\% assessed value change within three years, with wide variance across
jurisdictions. For wildfire-affected counties, property tax responses therefore
concentrate in the 2017 and 2022 census years (four to nine years post-fire for
2013--2016 fires), capturing medium-run assessments. Near-zero property tax effects
(Section~5) are consistent with this assessment lag and the offsetting mechanisms
that operate in home rule jurisdictions.

\textbf{Staggered DiD methodology.} Standard two-way fixed-effects (TWFE) estimators
are biased when treatment effects differ across cohorts or calendar time
\citep{baker2022, sun2021}. Because counties enter treatment across 2013--2021 and
fiscal effects may differ by cohort (early-treated counties observed through 2022,
late-treated observed once), we require heterogeneity-robust estimators.
Our primary specification uses the Callaway and Sant'Anna \citeyearpar{callaway2021}
group-time estimator, which separately identifies cohort-specific effects and
aggregates them, avoiding implicit negative weights that bias standard TWFE. We
report the Sun-Abraham \citeyearpar{sun2021} heterogeneity-robust TWFE and
Synthetic DiD \citep{arkhangelsky2021} as robustness checks to assess whether
results depend on the control-group weighting scheme implicit in each estimator.
Pre-trend tests follow \citet{roth2022}.

\section{Data}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Sample Construction}

The sample comprises all county governments in the contiguous United States
(lower-48 states). We restrict to counties present in the Census of Governments
quinquennial census for all five years (2002, 2007, 2012, 2017, 2022). The lower-48
states contain approximately 3,000 county governments; we exclude two Montana
consolidated city-counties (Deer Lodge and Silver Bow) that lack county-government
status in the CoG entity type system, yielding a balanced panel of 2,975 counties
and 14,875 county-year observations.

We implement two treatment definitions that correspond to the extensive and intensive
margins of wildfire exposure.

\textit{Extensive margin (fire incidence).} Treatment is a county's first qualifying
MTBS wildfire ($\geq\!1{,}000$ acres) within the 2013--2021 window, regardless of
severity. Under this definition, 884 counties are treated: 629 experienced a first
qualifying fire in 2013--2016 (early cohort, g\,=\,2017; observed post-treatment in
the 2017 and 2022 censuses) and 255 experienced a first qualifying fire in 2017--2021
(late cohort, g\,=\,2022; observed in the 2022 census). The 2,091 counties with no
qualifying MTBS fire in 2013--2021 form the never-treated control group.

\textit{Intensive margin (fire severity).} Treatment is a county's first
\textit{high-severity} qualifying wildfire within the 2013--2021 window. A fire
qualifies as high-severity if MTBS analysts identified a classifiable High-severity
dNBR tail in the fire's burn-severity histogram. Operationally, this is indicated by
the fire-specific \texttt{HIGH\_THRES} attribute (100--5,000 dNBR units) in the MTBS
perimeter file, representing the dNBR threshold above which pixels reflect severe
vegetation mortality and stand-replacement burning. Fires with
\texttt{HIGH\_THRES}\,=\,9,999 lack a detectable high-severity tail and are
classified as low-to-moderate-severity. Under this definition, 384 counties are
treated: 267 in the early cohort (g\,=\,2017) and 117 in the late cohort (g\,=\,2022).
The control group comprises 2,591 counties without a qualifying high-severity fire
in 2013--2021, including those that experienced only low-to-moderate-severity fires.
After propensity-score inverse-probability weighting in the doubly-robust estimation
(see Section~4), this control group has an effective sample size of 122.1 reweighted
units, reflecting concentration on high-fire-risk counties comparable to treated
counties.

The intensive-margin control group is strictly larger than the extensive-margin
control group: it includes all 2,091 zero-fire counties plus counties with
low-to-moderate-severity fires. These counties provide an expanded, closer
counterfactual for high-severity-fire counties because they share similar wildfire
hazard profiles and pre-treatment fire histories with the treated group while
differing primarily on fire severity.

\subsection{Fiscal Outcomes}

Fiscal data come from the Census of Governments quinquennial census, which provides
complete coverage of all government units. For the 2017 and 2022 censuses, which
use the Individual Unit File format (a 32-character fixed-width record), aggregate
summary codes (total general revenue, total general expenditure) are absent.
We reconstruct these aggregates from line-item codes following the published
itemization guide, summing across federal and state intergovernmental revenue
(B-codes and C-codes), own-source revenue (T, U, and A codes), and direct general
expenditure (E, F, G, and J codes).

All fiscal outcomes are per capita in 2019 dollars, using ACS 5-year population
estimates and national CPI-U deflation. For calendar-year and July--June fiscal-year
states, fiscal year ending June 30, 2017 is assigned to calendar year 2016, aligning
fiscal data with the pre-treatment baseline. For December-fiscal-year states
(Indiana, Kentucky, Minnesota, Missouri, New Hampshire, New Jersey, New York,
North Dakota, Ohio, Pennsylvania, South Dakota, West Virginia, and Wisconsin), the
CoG-labeled census year is used directly. Treatment cohort assignments, based on
fire year rather than fiscal year, are unaffected by state-level fiscal year
differences.

The headline outcome is \textit{fiscal balance per capita} (total general revenues
minus total general expenditures), which captures the net fiscal burden in a
single directly interpretable unit and maps to the \citet{liao2022} budget deficit
probability result. Secondary outcomes decompose the fiscal balance into revenue
(total revenue, property tax revenue, intergovernmental transfers) and
expenditure (total expenditures, capital outlays) components.

\subsection{Wildfire Hazard and Treatment}

The primary matching variable is the 2012 USFS Wildfire Potential (WFP) raster
\citep[RDS-2015-0045]{scott2013}, which provides a continuous
index of wildfire likelihood and intensity at 270m resolution. The 2012 vintage is
predetermined for all fires from 2013 onward: it was finalized before the 2013 fire
season and reflects FSim fire modeling inputs calibrated on data through 2011.
County-level WFP is the area-weighted mean of raster cells intersecting the county
boundary, computed in the Albers Equal Area projection (EPSG:5070). WFP values
in the sample range from near-zero (urban or desert counties) to over 1,000 in
high-hazard forested counties.

Fire perimeters, treatment assignment, and severity attributes come from the
Monitoring Trends in Burn Severity (MTBS) database, which provides satellite-derived
fire perimeters for all fires exceeding 1,000 acres in the western US from 1984
onward. Each MTBS fire record includes the \texttt{HIGH\_THRES} attribute: the
fire-specific dNBR threshold above which pixels are classified as High-severity.
This threshold is set separately for each fire using the fire's dNBR histogram;
a value of 9,999 indicates the histogram lacked a classifiable High-severity tail,
meaning high-severity burning either did not occur or could not be isolated.
We intersect fire perimeters with county boundaries using GeoPandas and assign each
county the year of its first qualifying \textit{high-severity} fire in 2013--2021.
Counties with no qualifying high-severity fire in this window are never-treated.
We construct a 100 km smoke exclusion buffer around all fire perimeters. For fiscal
outcomes, which operate through property damage and local spending channels rather
than the smoke inhalation channel, the buffer restriction is not applied in the
main analysis.

\subsection{Covariates}

Propensity score estimation uses baseline covariates measured at the 2012 CoG census
or 2007--2011 ACS: WFP quintile, pre-2013 fire indicator and log cumulative acres
burned, USDA Rural-Urban Continuum Code (2013 vintage), log median household income,
poverty rate, population share aged 65 or older, and log population density.
We exclude health insurance coverage (ACS \texttt{B27001}) because it is unavailable
in pre-2012 ACS 5-year files, preventing consistent pre-treatment matching. We
include pre-treatment fiscal baselines (2012 log revenue, log property tax, and log
long-term debt per capita) to account for fiscal differences that may predict both
wildfire exposure and outcomes.

\subsection{Summary Statistics}

Table~\ref{tab:balance} reports pre-treatment means and standardized mean differences
(SMD) for treated (high-severity fire) and control (no high-severity fire) counties,
before and after propensity-score inverse-probability weighting. In the unweighted
comparison, treated counties have substantially higher wildfire potential (WFP mean
826 vs.\ 79; SMD = 0.93), higher rates of pre-2013 fire history (93.5\% vs.\ 30.1\%;
SMD = 1.72), and higher pre-2013 log fire acres (SMD = 1.55). After IPW, WFP quintile
compresses to SMD = 0.06, and pre-2013 fire history is well-balanced (SMD = $-$0.02).
The national control group of 2,591 counties provides a large, diverse comparison
set. After propensity-score IPW, the effective sample size (ESS) is 122.1 reweighted
control units---roughly one-third the treated count of 384, reflecting concentration
of weights on high-fire-risk counties that most closely resemble treated counties.

\section{Empirical Strategy}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Estimating Equation}

The primary estimator is the group-time average treatment effect (ATT) of
\citet{callaway2021}. Let $g \in \{2017, 2022\}$ denote the first calendar year
in which county $i$'s treatment cohort first appears post-treatment in a CoG
census year, and let $\infty$ denote never-treated. The group-time ATT is:
%
\begin{equation}
  ATT(g, t) \;=\; \mathbb{E}\!\left[Y_{it}(g) - Y_{it}(\infty) \mid G_i = g\right]
  \label{eq:att_gt}
\end{equation}
%
\noindent where $Y_{it}(g)$ is the potential outcome for county $i$ in census year
$t$ under first treatment in cohort $g$, and $Y_{it}(\infty)$ is the never-treated
potential outcome. We estimate equation~(\ref{eq:att_gt}) using the doubly-robust
(DR) estimator of \citet{callaway2021}, which combines propensity-score and
outcome-regression adjustments so that the estimate is consistent if either
the propensity score or the outcome model is correctly specified.

The propensity score model is logistic regression of ever-treated status on
covariates described in Section~3. The outcome regression includes the same covariate
vector. We specify \texttt{base\_period = ``varying''} to set a separate
pre-treatment comparison period for each cohort-time cell, and
\texttt{control\_group = ``nevertreated''} to restrict comparisons to the 2,591
never-treated counties. Standard errors are clustered by county using block bootstrap
with 999 replications.

The primary reported estimate is the simple aggregated ATT:
%
\begin{equation}
  \widehat{ATT} \;=\; \sum_{g,t:\,t \geq g} \hat{\omega}(g,t) \cdot \widehat{ATT}(g,t)
  \label{eq:att_simple}
\end{equation}
%
\noindent where $\hat{\omega}(g,t) \propto \Pr(G_i = g)$ weights each cohort-period
cell by cohort size. We also report cohort-specific aggregations (ATT for
g\,=\,2017 and g\,=\,2022 separately) and event-study dynamic aggregations
(ATT at relative time $\ell = t - g$ for $\ell \in \{-10, -5, 0, 5\}$ in
quinquennial CoG years).

\subsection{Identifying Assumption}
\label{sec:identify}

Identification rests on conditional parallel trends: conditional on WFP quintile,
pre-treatment fire history, and other covariates, treated and never-treated counties
would have followed the same fiscal trajectory absent wildfire. Within a given
wildfire-hazard quintile, fire occurrence is triggered by stochastic weather
shocks---drought, lightning, wind---that are orthogonal to a county's underlying
fiscal trend. By matching on WFP baseline hazard, we compare treated and control
counties with similar fire risk, isolating the causal effect of the shock's timing.

\subsection{Pre-trends and Power}
\label{sec:pretrends}

We test for pre-treatment parallel trends using the dynamic ATT estimates at
$\ell = -10$ and $\ell = -5$ (census years 2002 and 2007 for the g\,=\,2017 cohort).
A joint chi-squared test of the null that all pre-treatment dynamic ATTs equal
zero is reported for each outcome. Pre-trend tests pass for all headline fiscal outcomes except intergovernmental
revenues, which shows a joint pre-trend $p = 0.042$. For fiscal balance, total
revenue, and capital expenditures, joint pre-trend tests are not rejected
(all $p > 0.10$). The intergovernmental revenue pre-trend violation is addressed
via Honest DiD sensitivity analysis (Rambachan and Roth 2023) in
Section~\ref{sec:robustness}; the post-treatment estimate remains consistent with
zero under all magnitudes of pre-trend misspecification. The strong pre-trend
performance for the main fiscal outcomes reflects the improved comparability of the
expanded national control group.

A critical limitation: passing pre-trend tests does not validate conditional parallel
trends when pre-treatment variation is limited \citep{roth2022}. This application
has only three pre-treatment census observations (2002, 2007, 2012). The maximum
undetected pre-trend slope (1.96 times the pre-period bootstrap SE, a conservative
scalar bound from \citealt{roth2022}) is approximately \$795 per capita per
quinquennium for fiscal balance---roughly twice the ATT point estimate of \$+379---and
\$289 for capital expenditures, comparable to the ATT of \$\!-293. Pre-treatment
tests pass, but with only three pre-treatment periods they cannot confirm conditional
parallel trends, only show consistency with them.
The robustness section addresses this concern via Honest DiD sensitivity analysis
(Rambachan and Roth 2023).

\subsection{Threats to Identification}

\textbf{Property assessment lag.} Property tax revenues respond to wildfire with a
multi-year lag: assessors in most western states reassess on two- to three-year
cycles, and California's Proposition 8 calamity reassessment provisions create
a California-specific mechanism that allows immediate reassessment at owner request.
The quinquennial CoG census captures fiscal outcomes roughly four to nine years
post-fire for g\,=\,2017 counties and one to five years post-fire for g\,=\,2022
counties, ensuring that medium-run tax revenue responses are captured. The
g\,=\,2022 cohort, observed in only one post-treatment census year, provides
limited power for this cohort.

\textbf{FEMA endogeneity.} Federal disaster declarations are endogenously triggered by
fire severity, creating a mechanical correlation between fire damage and
intergovernmental revenue through FEMA Hazard Mitigation and Individual Assistance
programs. Intergovernmental revenue is therefore treated as a mechanism outcome
rather than a control variable. The main specification for fiscal balance uses
total revenue net of all intergovernmental transfers, isolating own-source
fiscal capacity.

\textbf{Propensity score specification.} The propensity score is a logistic
regression of ever-treated status on the covariate vector $\mathbf{X}$.
Separate propensity score models are estimated for the extensive margin
(ever-treated = any qualifying fire) and the intensive margin (ever-treated =
any high-severity fire). The C\&S doubly-robust estimator uses these
margin-specific PS weights together with an outcome regression to construct
the ATT. Standard errors are obtained by block bootstrap over counties.

\textbf{Spatial autocorrelation.} County clustering of standard errors accounts
for within-county serial correlation but not for cross-county spatial correlation.
Adjacent western counties share watershed boundaries and face common economic
shocks (regional drought, statewide fiscal rules) that may correlate their fiscal
outcomes. Spatial HAC standard errors would likely be larger than the reported
block-bootstrap SEs; this limitation biases toward finding significance and thus
works against the null result reported here.

\textbf{Control pool composition and smoke contamination.} By expanding the
never-treated group to include low-to-moderate-severity-fire counties, we compare
counties on a common fire-exposure continuum rather than comparing fire-exposed to
fire-free counties. This design choice improves covariate balance but shifts the
identification concern: if low-severity-fire counties also experience fiscal impacts,
our estimates will understate the high-severity effect---an attenuation bias where
the ATT captures high-severity relative to low-severity fire, not high-severity
relative to no fire. The estimated ATT therefore represents a lower bound on the
fiscal impact of high-severity wildfire; it is a locally identified but
policy-relevant parameter, since many risk-reduction interventions (prescribed
burns, fuel treatments) aim to reduce fire severity rather than fire occurrence.

\section{Results}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Extensive Margin: Fire Incidence}

Table~\ref{tab:main_ext} reports the extensive-margin results: ATTs comparing 884
counties that experienced a first qualifying wildfire ($\geq\!1{,}000$ acres) in
2013--2021 to 2,091 never-fired counties. All outcomes are statistically
indistinguishable from zero. Column (1), fiscal balance, shows
an ATT of \$+535 per capita (SE = 489, $p = 0.274$; 95\% CI: [\$\!-424, \$1,493]).
The positive sign reflects near-zero revenue changes combined with a decline in
total expenditures (\$\!-205, $p = 0.546$). Revenue outcomes are small in
magnitude: total revenue ATT = \$+330 (SE = 757, $p = 0.663$), property tax ATT = \$+341
(SE = 591, $p = 0.564$), intergovernmental transfers ATT = \$+53 (SE = 161, $p = 0.740$).
Capital expenditures show ATT = \$\!-35 (SE = 201, $p = 0.861$), consistent with
no effect at the extensive margin. Long-term debt is positive but imprecise
(\$+1,608, $p = 0.392$).

The extensive-margin null finding's credibility is limited by large covariate
imbalance: pre-treatment WFP is 826 for treated vs.\ 79 for controls
(SMD\,=\,0.93 unweighted), reflecting strong geographic sorting between high-hazard
and fire-free counties. Although doubly-robust reweighting partially addresses this
imbalance, the large baseline difference undermines causal inference for any-fire
exposure. The intensive-margin specification avoids this problem by comparing
high-severity to low-to-moderate-severity counties with similar underlying fire risk.

\subsection{Intensive Margin: Fire Severity}

Table~\ref{tab:main_int} reports doubly-robust C\&S ATT estimates for the intensive
margin, comparing 384 counties that experienced a first high-severity wildfire
in 2013--2021 to 2,591 counties without a qualifying high-severity fire (ESS = 122.1).
The headline intensive-margin finding is a capital expenditure decline: C\&S DR
yields \$\!-293 per capita (SE\,=\,164, $p = 0.075$; 95\% CI: [\$\!-615, \$29]).
The Sun-Abraham heterogeneity-robust TWFE estimator yields \$\!-87
(SE\,=\,44, $p = 0.049$), smaller in magnitude but conventionally significant.
Both estimators are negative and consistent with capital deferral, despite diverging
on magnitude due to different control-group weighting schemes (discussed in
Section~\ref{sec:robustness}). Total expenditures also decline:
ATT = \$\!-448 (SE = 274, $p = 0.102$).

Fiscal balance is positive and insignificant (ATT\,=\,\$+379, $p = 0.165$). The
positive sign reflects the specific channel: total expenditures decline by \$448
(SE\,=\,274, $p = 0.102$) while total revenue declines by only \$69
(SE\,=\,359, $p = 0.847$). Expenditure cuts exceed revenue losses, preserving fiscal balance; capital deferral
is the primary adjustment margin rather than revenue collapse or debt issuance. Property tax revenue is near zero (ATT\,=\,\$+40, $p = 0.853$). Property damage
causes assessed-value declines, but assessments lag market values by two to three
years (Section~2). Our 2017 and 2022 census observations fall four to nine years
post-fire for early-treated cohorts, suggesting medium-run assessments should be
captured. The null property-tax result indicates that either fire damage to assessed
values is modest, or offsetting mechanisms---levy adjustments in home rule
counties---are active (Section~\ref{sec:home_rule}). Intergovernmental transfers are
near zero (ATT\,=\,\$\!-40, $p = 0.798$), inconsistent with systematic FEMA aid
offset. Long-term debt is positive but
imprecise (\$+1,014, $p = 0.240$).

The intensive-margin design offers cleaner identification: covariate balance is strong
(WFP SMD after IPW\,=\,0.06, compared to 0.93 unweighted), pre-treatment parallel
trends are satisfied for all headline outcomes (Section~\ref{sec:pretrends}), and
the effective control pool is sizable (ESS\,=\,122.1 of 2,591). The capital
expenditure finding reflects post-fire fiscal triage: counties cut the most
deferrable budget item to fund emergency expenditures without raising revenues. At
the four-to-nine-year observation window, property tax revenues have not collapsed,
consistent with assessment lags and home-rule offsetting mechanisms (Section~\ref{sec:home_rule}).

\subsection{Cohort Heterogeneity}
\label{sec:cohort}

Table~\ref{tab:cohort} disaggregates the ATT by treatment cohort for both margins.
Panel~A (extensive margin) shows that g\,=\,2017 counties carry a fiscal balance ATT
of \$+642 (SE = 535, $p = 0.230$) and capital outlays ATT of \$\!-33 (SE = 248,
$p = 0.895$). For g\,=\,2022 counties, fiscal balance is near zero (\$+8,
$p = 0.974$) and capital outlays are \$\!-48 ($p = 0.775$). Across both cohorts,
the extensive-margin results are uniformly null.

Panel~B (intensive margin) shows more informative cohort-level estimates.
The g\,=\,2017 cohort (267 counties; fires 2013--2016) shows: fiscal balance ATT of
\$+551 (SE\,=\,305, $p = 0.071$), capital outlays ATT of \$\!-298 (SE\,=\,176,
$p = 0.089$), and total expenditures ATT of \$\!-605 (SE\,=\,312, $p = 0.053$).
The expenditure decline outpaces revenue changes (total revenue near zero), so
counties absorb fire-driven costs primarily through capital deferral rather than
revenue recovery, and fiscal balance remains positive.
The g\,=\,2022 cohort (117 counties; fires 2017--2021) is observed in only the 2022
census---one post-treatment observation. The capital outlay estimate
(\$\!-268, SE\,=\,149, $p = 0.072$) relies entirely on covariate adjustment rather
than within-county pre-post variation, limiting causal interpretation. The estimate
is directionally consistent with the g\,=\,2017 result (\$\!-298), with both cohorts
showing capital deferral of \$270--\$300 per capita, suggesting this adjustment
occurs within four to nine years of fire exposure regardless of cohort entry year.

\subsection{Event Study}

Figure~\ref{fig:event_study} plots dynamic ATT estimates at event times
$\ell \in \{-10, -5, 0, 5\}$ for fiscal balance and capital expenditures under
the intensive-margin specification. Pre-treatment estimates at $\ell = -10$ and
$\ell = -5$ are statistically indistinguishable from zero for both outcomes, and
the joint pre-trend test is not rejected (all $p > 0.50$ across headline outcomes).
Post-treatment, capital expenditures decline at $\ell = 0$ and persist at $\ell = 5$,

\subsection{Dose-Response: Does Fire Size Predict Fiscal Effects?}
\label{sec:dose_response}

The preceding results compare counties that experienced any qualifying fire against
those that did not. If fire incidence alone yields no detectable effect, very large
fires may still exceed typical county reserves enough to generate measurable fiscal
damage. To examine this, we construct a dose variable,
$D_i = \log(\text{MaxAcres}_i)$, equal to the log of the largest qualifying
MTBS fire experienced by county $i$ within its treatment cohort window
(2013--2016 for cohort $g = 2017$; 2017--2021 for cohort $g = 2022$).
The median $D_i$ among treated counties corresponds to approximately 5,825 acres.

The primary dose-response estimator is a two-way fixed effects regression,
\begin{equation}
  Y_{it} = \alpha_i + \gamma_t + \beta \, (D_i \times \text{Post}_{it}) + \varepsilon_{it},
  \label{eq:dose_twfe}
\end{equation}
where $\text{Post}_{it} = \mathbf{1}[\text{year\_cog}_t \geq g_i]$ for treated counties
and zero for never-treated counties. Unit and year fixed effects absorb all
time-invariant county characteristics and common time trends. Standard errors are
clustered by county. The coefficient $\beta$ captures the additional post-treatment
change in fiscal outcomes per unit increase in $\log(\text{MaxAcres})$, relative to
the never-treated baseline. This TWFE specification is subject to the same
heterogeneous-treatment-effects bias as standard TWFE in staggered designs
\citep{baker2022}: if dose-response slopes differ between the g\,=\,2017 and
g\,=\,2022 cohorts, the estimator may assign implicit negative weights to some
cohort-period combinations. Panel~B presents Callaway-Sant'Anna dose-bin estimates as a complementary check that
avoids this concern.

Panel A of Table~\ref{tab:dose_response} presents TWFE dose-response estimates for
all seven fiscal outcomes. A 10-fold increase in maximum fire size (e.g., 10,000 to
100,000 acres) implies a fiscal balance change of
$\log(10) \times \hat{\beta} \approx 2.3 \times 10.23 \approx \$24$ per capita
(0.6\% of median pre-fire balance)---substantively negligible. The fiscal balance
$\hat{\beta} = +10.23$ (SE\,=\,4.80, $p = 0.033$) is statistically significant but
economically trivial. More important, pre-trend tests for total revenue, capital
expenditures, and total expenditures are rejected ($p \leq 0.040$), indicating that
TWFE dose estimates for these outcomes are biased by differential pre-treatment
trends across the fire-size distribution. The failure of pre-trend tests for total
revenue, capital outlays, and total expenditures indicates that dose-response slopes
differ across the fire-size distribution. The fiscal balance pre-trend passes
($p = 0.313$), but the pattern of failures for its components (revenue $p = 0.089$,
expenditure $p = 0.003$) suggests confounding: any fiscal balance signal may reflect
omitted fire-size-correlated differences rather than true dose effects. The C\&S
dose-bin results are more credible.

Panel B provides the more robust Callaway-Sant'Anna dose-bin check using the same
covariate formula and bootstrap clustering as the main analysis. We split treated
counties at the median $D_i$ (5,825 acres) into low-dose ($n = 442$) and
high-dose ($n = 442$) bins and estimate the simple ATT for each bin against
2,091 never-treated controls. Both bins are statistically indistinguishable from
zero across all outcomes. Low-dose counties: fiscal balance ATT = \$+523
(SE = \$481, $p = 0.277$); capital outlays ATT = \$+23 (SE = \$191, $p = 0.906$).
High-dose counties: fiscal balance ATT = \$+332 (SE = \$464, $p = 0.474$);
capital outlays ATT = \$\!-110 (SE = \$132, $p = 0.407$). The C\&S dose-bin
design finds no evidence of a dose-response relationship at any point in the
fire-size distribution, consistent with the overall null result at the extensive
margin.

Taken together, the dose-response evidence is mixed. The TWFE fiscal balance
estimate is statistically significant with clean pre-trends, but the pattern of
pre-trend failures for related outcomes ($p < 0.10$ for two outcomes, $p < 0.01$
for total expenditures) raises concern about differential selection into fire
size. The C\&S dose-bin design, which is robust to this concern, finds uniformly
null effects. We interpret the dose-bin null as the more reliable indicator: fire
size does not predict fiscal outcomes in the staggered DiD framework.

\subsection{Institutional Heterogeneity: Home Rule vs.\ Dillon's Rule}
\label{sec:home_rule}

County fiscal responses to wildfire depend partly on legal authority over tax policy
and debt. Dillon's Rule states limit counties to explicitly delegated powers; home
rule states grant counties residual fiscal authority. Dillon's Rule should constrain
the fiscal response channel: if revenue adjustment is disabled by law (e.g., no
discretion to raise property tax levies after assessed-value erosion), counties must
absorb fire costs through expenditure cuts or debt issuance. Home rule counties can
adjust levies. If capital outlays are the most deferrable budget item, Dillon's Rule
counties should show more capital compression than home rule counties.

Table~\ref{tab:home_rule} presents C\&S doubly-robust ATTs estimated separately
for counties in Dillon's Rule states (n\,=\,2{,}177; 166 intensive-treated)
and home rule states (n\,=\,798; 218 intensive-treated), using the same
intensive-margin treatment and covariate formula as the main specification
(Table~\ref{tab:main_int}).

The results reveal a striking asymmetry in property tax revenues. In Dillon's
Rule states, high-severity wildfire reduces property tax revenues per capita
by \$410 (SE = 161, $p = 0.011$). In home rule states, the estimated effect
is near zero and statistically indistinguishable from zero ($-$\$26, SE = 242,
$p = 0.915$). Dillon's Rule counties hold only explicitly delegated taxing powers
and cannot discretionarily offset wildfire-induced assessed-value declines; home
rule counties can adjust levies or use alternative revenue instruments.

Capital expenditure compression is directionally consistent across both groups
(Dillon's Rule: $-$\$133, SE = 114, $p = 0.243$; home rule: $-$\$21,
SE = 356, $p = 0.952$), though neither subgroup estimate achieves conventional
significance, partly due to reduced effective sample size within each subgroup.
The point estimates are consistent with a general compression dynamic that
operates independently of legal doctrine, while the property tax channel is
specific to fiscally constrained Dillon's Rule jurisdictions.

\subsection{Alternative Control Group and Sample Restrictions}

Table~\ref{tab:robustness} presents eight robustness specifications using doubly-robust C\&S method for fiscal
balance, total revenue, and capital expenditures, applied separately to each
treatment definition. Panel~A covers the extensive margin (any qualifying fire
$\geq\!1{,}000$ acres); Panel~B covers the intensive margin (first high-severity
fire). Row 0 in each panel replicates the corresponding main result
(Tables~\ref{tab:main_ext} and \ref{tab:main_int}). Rows 1--7 apply identical
sample and specification changes to both margins, allowing direct comparison of
how each check affects the two designs. The discussion below describes each
specification; magnitudes cited are from Panel~B (intensive margin) unless
otherwise noted. Row 1 expands the control group to include not-yet-treated counties: g\,=\,2022
counties serve as controls for g\,=\,2017 counties in 2017, before the g\,=\,2022
counties are themselves treated. This specification shifts capital expenditure to
\$\!-233 (SE\,=\,111, $p = 0.037$, now conventionally significant) and fiscal
balance to \$+312 (SE\,=\,222, $p = 0.159$).

Row 2 drops outlier counties (those with total revenue per capita more than
three times the state-year median) as complete observations to maintain a
balanced panel. Capital expenditure estimates are virtually unchanged (\$\!-293,
$p = 0.047$), confirming that the result is not driven by extreme observations.
Row 3 restricts to eleven western states (AZ, CA, CO, ID, MT, NV, NM, OR, UT, WA,
WY)---the fire-prone region. The western results are striking: fiscal balance declines
by \$\!-1{,}252 (SE\,=\,403, $p = 0.002$) and total revenue by \$\!-1{,}063
(SE\,=\,445, $p = 0.017$), both highly significant. Capital outlays reverse sign to
\$+186 (SE\,=\,366, $p = 0.611$, null). This reversal indicates that the national capital expenditure decline (\$\!-293)
concentrates in western states; non-western counties show no capital compression,
and the national average is attenuated by the large unaffected eastern sample.
Row 4 substitutes the WHP 2014 raster for WFP 2012 in the propensity score;
capital expenditure shifts to \$\!-436 (SE = 411, $p = 0.289$), consistent in
direction with the baseline but with a wide confidence interval.
Row 5 excludes all California counties ($n = 52$ intensive-margin treated counties)
to test whether the national finding depends on California's Proposition~13
environment. Capital expenditures reverse sign to \$+97 (SE\,=\,104, $p = 0.349$),
statistically and economically null. This is a critical finding: the national capital
expenditure decline of \$\!-293 depends entirely on California counties. California's
combination of high fire severity and Proposition~13 reassessment constraints
(frozen assessed values, mandatory reassessment only upon sale) creates a fiscal
environment that does not generalize. Outside California, high-severity wildfire does
not compress capital outlays. This sensitivity requires qualification of the headline
result: the capital deferral effect is concentrated in California, not a general
national response.

Rows 6 and 7 address the property tax assessment-cycle concern
(Section~\ref{sec:identify}). For the intensive margin (Panel~B), both
annual-reassessment states (CA, OR, WA; 30 never-treated controls) and multi-year
states produce uniformly null estimates: the annual subgroup is severely
power-constrained, so the null does not rule out delayed effects; the multi-year null
(\$\!-76 for fiscal balance, \$+84 for capital outlays) is more informative.
For the extensive margin (Panel~A), the annual-reassessment split is not identified:
CA, OR, and WA counties that experienced no qualifying fire are nearly absent at
the extensive margin (almost all such counties have had at least one large fire),
yielding a near-zero never-treated control pool. Row~6 is therefore omitted for
Panel~A. The multi-year-cycle extensive result (row 7) is uniformly null across all
three outcomes (\$+206, \$+159, \$\!-38), confirming that the extensive-margin null
is not an artifact of states with long assessment delays.

Spatial spillovers to untreated counties pose a potential violation of the stable
unit treatment value assumption. However, the primary fiscal transmission channels
---property assessment and debt issuance---operate at the fire perimeter and do not
extend beyond county boundaries. Smoke-driven productivity effects on labor and
health are outside our scope. At national scale, treated and never-treated counties
are geographically dispersed; most untreated counties are far from any fire perimeter.
Cross-county spillovers through regional economic shocks are most plausible for
spatial neighbors; spatial clustering of standard errors (Section~4) addresses this
residual concern.

\subsection{Alternative Estimators}
\label{sec:robustness}

Table~\ref{tab:estimators} compares four estimators for the intensive-margin
specification. The extensive margin is uniformly null across all seven outcomes
and all robustness specifications (Panel~A of Table~\ref{tab:robustness}); the
estimator comparison is therefore most informative for the intensive margin, where
the estimates diverge. For capital expenditures---the headline result---C\&S doubly-robust
yields \$\!-293 (SE = 164, $p = 0.075$). Sun and Abraham \citeyearpar{sun2021}
yield \$\!-87 (SE = 44, $p = 0.049$), the only estimate with conventional
statistical significance. Synthetic DiD \citep{arkhangelsky2021} yields \$\!-3
(SE = 18, $p = 0.851$) and two-stage DiD \citep{gardner2022} yields \$\!-25
(SE = 18, $p = 0.170$).

For fiscal balance, the four estimators range from \$\!-12 (Sun-Abraham) to
\$+379 (C\&S DR), all statistically null. This sign instability reflects
control-group composition: the C\&S doubly-robust estimator reweights toward
high-WFP counties (predominantly western), recovering an estimate closer to the
\$\!-1{,}252 western effect seen in Row 3. Synthetic DiD and Two-Stage DiD weight
all never-treated counties equally, averaging in the unaffected eastern majority
and attenuating the estimate toward zero. The western-state subsample (Row 3) shows
that equal-weighting estimators were pulling toward zero by construction; C\&S's
reweighting scheme is more appropriate for this setting, concentrating comparisons
on fire-risk-matched counties.

The capital expenditure finding is the most consistent signal across estimators.
Two of four estimators (C\&S and Sun-Abraham) yield negative, marginally or
conventionally significant estimates; the other two are negative but imprecise.
No estimator shows a positive capital expenditure effect. This directional
consensus, absent for fiscal balance and revenue outcomes, supports interpreting
the capital expenditure decline as a real if imprecisely estimated effect.

\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
%% ============================================================

This paper provides causal estimates of wildfire fiscal effects on county governments
across the contiguous United States. Using Census of Governments data (2002--2022)
and staggered DiD designs matched on USFS Wildfire Potential, we estimate that
high-severity wildfires reduce capital expenditures by approximately \$87--\$293 per
capita nationally, with substantial heterogeneity: the effect is concentrated in
western and California counties and is absent in non-California states. Fiscal balance
and total revenues are statistically null nationally, masking opposing regional
effects. The extensive margin (any qualifying fire) and the dose-response margin
(fire size) generate no detectable effects.

The results support a \emph{fiscal triage} interpretation: faced with fire-driven
costs and constrained revenue options, counties defer capital outlays. Revenues
cannot easily be raised in the short run (Dillon's Rule constraints, delayed
assessment cycles), and federal aid is uncertain (FEMA declarations are endogenous
to fire severity). Faced with suppression reimbursements, debris removal, and
emergency response costs, counties cut the most deferrable item. Measured revenues
are stable and long-term debt rises modestly but insignificantly, leaving capital
deferral as the residual adjustment. The data show capital compression consistent
with this mechanism but do not directly observe emergency expenditure categories or
departmental reallocations. The effect is modest---roughly 8\% of the pre-treatment
capital expenditure median---and both C\&S DR (\$\!-293) and Sun-Abraham (\$\!-87)
point in the same direction despite different control-group weighting schemes,
supporting a real if imprecisely estimated effect.

Whether the revenue channel is activated depends on a county's statutory taxing
authority. In Dillon's Rule states, wildfire-induced property value erosion reduced
property tax revenues by \$410 per capita (SE = 161, $p = 0.011$). In home rule
states, the effect is near zero ($-$\$26, SE = 242, $p = 0.915$), suggesting that
counties with residual fiscal authority offset assessed-value losses through levy
adjustments. Nationally these opposing responses largely cancel, so the revenue null
does not mean fire leaves revenues unaffected; it is a weighted average of
heterogeneous institutional adjustments. The scope of delegated taxing authority
thus shapes how wildfire costs propagate into county budgets
(Table~\ref{tab:home_rule}).

In western states, fiscal balance declines by \$\!-1{,}252 ($p = 0.002$) and total
revenue by \$\!-1{,}063 ($p = 0.017$), driven by high fire severity and
California's Proposition~13 constraints on reassessment. Excluding California
eliminates the national capital expenditure finding (\$\!-293 becomes \$+97,
$p = 0.349$): the capital deferral effect is California-specific. Non-California
states show no capital compression despite experiencing high-severity fires.
California's fiscal response to wildfire is shaped by its property tax regime,
not by fire severity alone; capital deferral occurs where revenue recovery is
institutionally blocked, and revenue collapse where fire exposure and tax-base
rigidity are jointly high.

The dose-response analysis yields no dose gradient. The C\&S dose-bin design
yields null ATTs for both low-dose ($\leq$5,825 acres) and high-dose ($>$5,825 acres)
subsamples (all $p > 0.27$); fire size does not predict fiscal impacts within the
high-severity sample. A TWFE fiscal balance estimate is statistically significant
($\hat{\beta} = +10.23$ per log-acre, $p = 0.033$), but pre-trend violations for
expenditure outcomes ($p \leq 0.040$) undermine the TWFE specification, and we
treat the C\&S dose-bin null as the more credible result.

A critical limitation is that the national capital expenditure finding depends
entirely on California counties (Row 5 of Table~\ref{tab:robustness}). This
California-specific result may reflect Proposition~13's unique reassessment
constraints rather than a general wildfire-fiscal response. Future research should
examine why non-California states' fiscal responses to high-severity fire differ from
California's. Second, the five-year CoG census cadence prevents identification of
short-run adjustment dynamics within a cohort window; annual data would improve
precision. Third, the g\,=\,2022 cohort has only one post-treatment observation
(2022), limiting inference for that cohort. The Dillon's Rule finding is robust and
motivates further study of how statutory tax authority, assessment cycles, and
federal fire aid jointly determine local fiscal resilience to climate shocks.
